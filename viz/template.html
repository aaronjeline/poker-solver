Why hill climbing can't beat poker

The goal: order a 52-card deck so the dealer wins no matter where you cut it. A hill climber comes within a card or two for 2 players — then falls apart at 3 and 4. These charts show why: the search space is a field of short, isolated hills.

Here's the game. Take a fixed ordering of a standard 52-card deck. Someone cuts it at one of 52 positions (rotating the deck), then a hand of Texas Hold'em is dealt to n players. The dealer is player 0. A deck's score is the number of the 52 cut positions at which the dealer wins. A perfect deck scores 52/52: the dealer wins from every cut.

Steepest-ascent hill climbing tries to find such a deck: start from a random ordering, look at every deck reachable by swapping two cards (there are C(52,2) = 1326 of them), move to whichever swap wins the most games, and repeat. When no single swap improves the score, you're on a hilltop — a local optimum — and the climb restarts from scratch.

A note on method: the win/loss of every hand is looked up in a 1.2 GB precomputed table, so the search runs in Rust, not in your browser. This page just renders what the Rust viz command measured over many climbs. All data is embedded below — no network required.

01Every climb stalls below the summit

Each line is one climb: it starts at a random deck (left) and steps uphill until it gets stuck. For 2 players the climbs push high, brushing 52. Add players and the whole cloud sinks — at 4 players climbs die out in the 30s, nowhere near a perfect deck. Same algorithm, dramatically lower ceiling.

02The ceiling drops, the gap widens

Where do the climbs land? These are the distributions of final scores — one row per player count, sharing an axis. As n grows the whole distribution slides left and the distance to the 52 target grows. The share of climbs that actually reach a perfect deck falls to essentially zero.

03At the top, there is nowhere to go

What does being stuck actually look like? For any deck, its 1326 swap-neighbors split into three groups: some score better, some the same, some worse. A random deck has hundreds of uphill moves. A local optimum has, by definition, zero — every one of the 1326 swaps ties or loses ground. That's the trap.

04Uphill moves vanish as you climb

This is the mechanism behind the stall. As a climb's score rises (x-axis), the number of swaps that still improve it (y-axis) collapses toward zero. The higher the score, the rarer an uphill move — and for more players the supply of improving moves runs out sooner and lower, which is exactly why those climbs freeze in the 30s.

05The cuts it can't fix

Finally, a look at real decks. Each strip is 52 cells, one per cut position: blue where the dealer wins that cut, red where they lose (darker = bigger margin). A perfect deck would be all blue. The best deck the climber found still carries red cells — losing cuts that no single swap can repair without breaking a winning one.

06A landscape of countless peaks

So how many hills are there — and are they connected? Two facts settle it. First, across all these climbs, no two ever ended on the same deck: every restart discovers a brand-new summit, and those summits sit almost as far apart as two decks possibly can.

Second, are those summits joined by high ground or split by valleys? To find out we take ten of the summits the climbs found and walk a straight line between them through deck space — morphing one optimum into the next a single swap at a time, and measuring the score at every step. This is a probe, not the search: the climber itself stops dead at each summit and restarts from a fresh random deck — here we deliberately keep walking to see what lies between the peaks. The answer is the cross-section below. Between every pair of summits the score plunges into a deep valley — there is no ridgeline linking them. Each peak is an island.

08The genetic algorithm's broken toolbox

Hill climbing is crude — surely a genetic algorithm does better? It comes with a whole toolkit of five mutation operators, not just single swaps. But an operator is only useful if it can nudge a deck to a better score. Here's what each one actually does. Two of them break the search outright.

First, Rotation — "cut the deck at a random point" — can never change the score. The fitness already tallies all 52 cuts, so rotating the deck just relabels them: same games, same score, every time. A full fifth of the mutation budget is dead weight. Second, near a good deck every operator only makes things worse — and the "aggressive" ones (block swap, reversal, scramble) are wrecking balls, hurling the deck ten-plus wins downhill. None can make the small uphill step needed to reach a perfect deck.

09What actually works: beam search

Everything so far has been an autopsy of failure. So what works? Beam search — by doing the opposite of everything that failed. Instead of one climber it keeps a diverse population of 40 decks. Instead of only-uphill steps it uses simulated annealing, which accepts the occasional downhill move to climb back out of a trap. And instead of counting only wins — a number that sits dead flat across the plateaus of view 3 — it follows a margin-refined score that still has a gradient when the win count doesn't. Watch it break through the ceiling that stopped every hill climber.

But why can it move when a hill climber can't? Here's the trick, and it's the payoff of view 3. Take a deck stuck at a local optimum: zero of its 1326 swaps improve the win count — that's what "stuck" means. But score those same neighbors by margin — how comfortably the dealer wins or loses each cut — and a handful suddenly point uphill. That faint gradient, invisible to a win-counter, is the thread the annealer follows out.

The picture is consistent across the first eight views: this landscape is rugged — packed with local optima — and it gets ruggeder with more players. Steepest-ascent hill climbing is a purely local move; it can only ever walk to the nearest hilltop, and here the nearest hilltop is almost never the summit. A genetic algorithm was supposed to escape that, but its operators fight the problem instead of fitting it — one can't change the score at all, and the rest only demolish good decks. What finally works (view 9) does none of those things: it keeps a diverse population, accepts downhill moves to escape traps, and — most importantly — optimizes a score with a gradient instead of a flat win-count. Match the moves to the terrain, and even a landscape this rugged gives way.
